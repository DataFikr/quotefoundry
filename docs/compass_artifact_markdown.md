# Revenue-First Feature Roadmap for a Metal Fab / Welding Job Shop Micro-SaaS

## TL;DR
- **Build a fast, accurate quoting/estimating tool first** — it is the single feature small fab/welding shops (5–50 employees) will pay for immediately, because slow quoting directly loses revenue (a widely cited Lead Connect survey finds 78% of customers buy from the company that responds to their enquiry first) and bad quotes silently destroy margin. Everything else (scheduling, inventory, compliance) is a follow-on.
- **Win on "built for our workflow," instant value, and transparent low pricing** — incumbents (JobBOSS2, MRPeasy, Katana, Fishbowl, Prodsmart) are abandoned because they are bloated, require a full-time data-entry person, charge per-floor-user fees that feel abusive, and take months to implement. A solo founder beats them by being narrow, live in days, and priced predictably under the ~$10–15K upfront pain threshold.
- **Add weld/material compliance documentation as the #2 paid module** — welder continuity logs (AWS D1.1 / ASME IX 6-month rule) and mill-cert (MTR) traceability are recurring, audit-driven pains shops are demonstrably willing to pay software to solve, and they create stickiness because they are legally required and recurring.

## Key Findings

**1. The #1 convertible pain is quoting speed + quoting accuracy, not "ERP."** Across welding forums, machine-shop forums, and vendor case studies, the most repeated frustration is that estimating is slow, manual (spreadsheets/pen-and-paper), inconsistent, and loses both jobs and margin. Owners say they quote "100% by hand," that changing a quantity or material means redoing the whole quote, and that "a few missed quotes or botched orders" easily exceed the cost of good software. Speed is independently revenue-linked: the often-cited Lead Connect figure is that 78% of buyers purchase from whoever responds first, while only ~7% of B2B sales teams respond within five minutes — and most fab shops take 3–5 business days to quote.

**2. Incumbents are abandoned for five consistent reasons:** (a) **bloat** — a low-volume/high-mix job shop owner on Practical Machinist who ran E2 then JobBoss said it was "just bloated with a ton of stuff you pay for but don't use"; (b) **labor burden** — the same owner has "zero interest in bringing in a full time employee just for data entry to keep the ERP happy"; (c) **poor fit for high-mix custom/job work** — a custom-machine OEM "abandoned an upgrade to an inventory based ERP system because it couldn't be adapted to our job based production"; (d) **price** — small shops balk at $10–15K upfront and resent per-user floor fees; (e) **trust/implementation risk** — owners cite "industry leading options bought and abandoned in frustration," plus brutal JobBOSS2/E2 implementation horror stories (one Software Connect reviewer documented ~$37K spent before abandoning).

**3. Pricing sensitivity is sharp and patterned.** Small shops (sub-15 employees) repeatedly balk at $10–15K upfront ERP investments and "$50+/user just to punch in." MRPeasy's transparent ~$49–149/user/month, no-contract model is the widely praised value benchmark; Katana is actively abandoned over aggressive price hikes (one 6-year customer described being re-charged ~$899/month and a punitive cancellation) and migrating to a tool at "roughly ¼ of the cost." What makes them cancel: paying for unused modules, surprise increases, per-user fees on floor staff, and software they bought but never adopted ("$4K into J/B we don't use"). What makes them stay: it does the daily job, integrates with QuickBooks, and the record-keeping becomes the system of record.

**4. Compliance documentation is a real, recurring, willing-to-pay pain — but a specific kind.** The two that bite small structural/fab shops hardest: (a) **welder continuity logs** — AWS D1.1 Clause 4.2.3.1 / ASME IX require a welder use each qualified process at least once per 6 months or the qualification lapses; lapses mean costly re-tests, and per The Fabricator's CWI columnist the continuity report "is the thing that will sting you in an audit." (b) **material traceability / mill certs (MTRs)** — linking heat numbers on mill certs to finished parts, and producing a cert pack on demand for customer audits. ISO 9001 (Clause 8.5.2 traceability, 7.1.5 calibration, 8.6 receiving inspection) is the umbrella, but per Kaizen ISO Consulting the documentation burden for a small shop is "genuinely manageable — typically 15–25 documents," so a tool should target the specific recurring gaps (continuity, MTR linkage, calibration due-dates, NCR/CAPA) rather than full QMS.

**5. Market timing is favorable.** Per IoT Analytics' MES Market Report 2025–2031 (published Dec 2025), 54% of small- and medium-sized plants still use some combination of pen, paper, or spreadsheets as their MES; the global MES market reached $5.5 billion in 2024 (~6.5% of the $85 billion industrial software market), just 8% of plants use a commercial MES, and the global leader holds less than 10% share — a fragmented, underserved SMB opportunity. Per Gartner, by 2025 70% of new applications will use low-code/no-code (up from <25% in 2020), and there is explicit funded movement toward small-manufacturer tooling: per TechCrunch (Mar 19, 2025), Paris-based PLM startup Aletiq raised a €6 million round (~$6.5M) led by Point Nine to serve small manufacturers.

## Details

### 1. Pain points (what they say they need most)
- **Quoting/estimating is the loudest, most universal pain.** Welding-forum owners ask repeatedly for "usable quoting software for the 1 or 2 man shop," describe "wasting countless hours entering profile weights onto spreadsheets," and an estimator/PM says "the juggling between estimating and managing is getting out of hand." A Practical Machinist owner acquiring a shop says response time is "too slow, I believe it is losing us business."
- **Job costing / estimate-vs-actual.** Shops can't easily tie real labor + machine time + material + overhead to a job; QuickBooks doesn't do real job costing, multi-level BOMs, or routing, so shops "export to Excel and rebuild the missing pieces by hand."
- **Scheduling / capacity visibility.** Owners want to tell a customer a delivery date "based more in fact than in optimism." But per The Fabricator, scheduling chaos is often a symptom of upstream disorganization, so a pure scheduler may underdeliver unless tied to real job data.
- **Job/status tracking and lost paperwork.** A 3-person/2-lathe shop owner: "I'm sick of hand writing everything and papers getting lost." Customers call for status updates; owners want real-time "where's my job" visibility.

### 2. Competitor gaps (by product)
- **JobBOSS2 (ECI):** Powerful but "difficult to learn and use," compared by reviewers to "older DOS-based systems"; weak/rigid reporting (needs SQL or paid custom reports); finicky web app that logs users out; one Capterra reviewer reported quoting got 20–35% slower after an update; post-ECI support complaints; expensive, painful implementations. Owner-reported cost ~$10–12K range; one shop documented ~$37K and abandonment.
- **MRPeasy:** Best value benchmark and transparent per-user pricing, but "logic for some calculations not intuitive"; high order-count shops get bumped into higher tiers regardless of revenue; one Capterra reviewer said paying "$50+ USD per worker just to have them 'punch in/out'" is steep when "we don't use 70% of the features," explicitly wishing for a "light license" for floor workers — a clear product opening.
- **Katana:** Loved for UI/ease, but not built for custom job/ETO work (designed around products/SKUs and e-commerce), higher entry price ($179–359/mo+ with features behind paywalls), and actively abandoned over aggressive price hikes (~$899/mo re-charges) and punitive cancellation terms.
- **Fishbowl:** A G2 reviewer called it "better than a spreadsheet, but not by much"; rigid workflows "more for large production of the same product" ("our company never makes the same two items"); free reports "useless" with custom reports costing $150–$2,000; perpetual licenses $4,300–6,500/user.
- **Prodsmart (Autodesk Fusion Operations):** Affordable MES entry point but per a Software Connect reviewer the scheduling module "is not intuitive" — can't auto-adjust to changes, no machine/worker availability, "blind when it comes to forecasting any type of ETA"; the reviewer concluded "as a start-up it was a good program, but it was not built for a growing, more advanced operation." Most positive reviews skew to non-US, non-metal customers.
- **Universal gap:** None of these is "built for our workflow" of high-mix, make-to-order, custom weldments/structural steel without a heavy data-entry tax.

### 3. Revenue-first features (what converts trial → paid)
**Converts fast (pay immediately):**
1. **Quote-in-minutes engine** with shop-specific rates (material markup, labor/burden rates, setup vs. run time, secondary ops, finishing), repeat-quote cloning, and professional branded PDF output. This is the clearest "pay today" feature — it directly wins jobs (speed) and protects margin (accuracy). Vendor data points: per Paperless Parts, quoting single components drops from up to 2 hours to 15 minutes and complex assemblies from up to 4 days to 4 hours; one shop cited going 30 min → 5 min; SecturaFAB/Tempus Tools cite "half a day → 30 minutes."
2. **Estimate-vs-actual job costing** — close the loop so the next quote is better; this is the retention hook because accuracy compounds with use.
3. **Realistic delivery-date / capacity view** tied to actual job load.

**Nice-to-have (later, lower urgency / weaker standalone conversion):**
- Full inventory/MRP, multi-level BOM automation, purchasing, advanced analytics dashboards, AI features. These sound good but don't drive the initial purchase decision for a 5–50 person shop and add the bloat/data-entry burden owners flee.

### 4. Compliance / quality features (which they'll pay for)
- **Welder continuity tracking (highest-value compliance feature):** auto-track each welder × process, alert before the 6-month lapse, store WPQ/WPS, generate audit-ready continuity logs. Re-tests are real money: Ironworkers Local 512 cites ~$500/recert at a training center; a shop with multiple welders × multiple processes faces compounding cost and lost availability. Existing point tools (ProWrite, Codeware Shopfloor, WPSAmerica, WeldTrace, Therness) prove willingness to pay — but they're often heavy/engineering-oriented, leaving room for a simple, alert-driven SMB tool.
- **Mill-cert / MTR traceability:** capture heat numbers, link certs to jobs/parts, one-click certificate pack for shipment/audit. Per Kaizen ISO Consulting, shops keep certs in "a Dropbox folder" with no link to finished parts — the exact gap auditors probe.
- **Calibration due-date tracking** (ISO 9001 7.1.5), **receiving inspection logs** (8.6), and **NCR/CAPA** — simple, recurring, audit-driven.
- **Positioning:** sell "audit-ready in an afternoon," not "QMS." Most small shops need ISO 9001 only because an OEM/government customer's supplier qualification demands it — frame compliance as winning/keeping customers, not bureaucracy.

### 5. Market demand signals (2024–2026)
- **IoT Analytics MES Market Report 2025–2031 (Dec 2025):** 54% of SMB plants still use pen/paper + spreadsheets; MES market ~$5.5B in 2024 (~6.5% of the $85B industrial software market), forecast to ~$8.7B by 2031; only 8% of plants use a commercial MES; the global leader holds <10% share; ">$50B addressable market." This is a large, fragmented, under-penetrated SMB opening.
- **SMB software market** broadly ~$72–77B in 2025–26 growing ~7% CAGR; SMEs are the fastest-growing segment, buying turnkey, fast-to-deploy apps. Per Gartner, 70% of new apps will use low-code/no-code by 2025 (up from <25% in 2020).
- **ABI Research (2H 2025):** heavy AI investment among PLM/MES vendors and explicit moves toward underserved small manufacturers — corroborated by Aletiq's ~$6.5M (€6M) raise in March 2025 (TechCrunch), where the CEO noted four players control ~90% of the PLM market.
- **Cloud dominates SMB** (~72% share) for low entry cost and fast deployment — favors a SaaS micro-product.

### 6. Pricing sensitivity (under $5M revenue shops)
- **Upfront pain threshold:** ~$10–15K all-in is where small shops balk; many waste $3–5K then abandon. As one owner put it, shops "don't flinch at buying fancy machine tools for 100k+, but we balk at spending 10,000 on something that is used every single day."
- **Per-user resentment:** floor "punch-in" users at $50+/user feel abusive; a cheap/free "floor license" tier is a wedge.
- **Benchmarks:** MRPeasy $49–149/user/mo, no contract (praised), plus a flat $79/mo per additional block of 10 users; xTuple ~$175/user/mo; Katana $179–899/mo (resented hikes); Steelhead from ~$500/mo (2-week deploy, ~10-min operator training); ProShop quote-based (~$45–50K for 46 employees, owner-reported); Global Shop ~$1,500/mo min 10 users; JobBOSS2 ~$10–12K range (owner-reported); industry-wide job-shop subscriptions $49–$715/user/mo, implementation typically 100–200% of annual software cost.
- **Churn drivers:** unused modules, surprise price hikes, per-user floor fees, never-adopted (no data-entry owner), poor support.
- **Retention drivers:** transparent pricing, no contract, QuickBooks integration, fast time-to-value, becoming the system of record.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 (launch, 0–6 months) — Win on quoting.** Ship a narrow, fast, fab-specific **quote/estimate tool**: shop-configurable rates, material markup, setup vs. run, secondary ops, repeat-quote cloning, branded PDF, QuickBooks export. Target time-to-first-quote of minutes and self-serve onboarding live in **days, not months**. Price transparently and below the balk threshold — e.g., flat **$99–249/mo** for the shop (not per floor user), no contract, free trial with no credit card. **Benchmark to change course:** if trial→paid conversion is below ~15–20% or activation (first real quote sent) takes more than one session, the tool isn't fast/narrow enough.

**Stage 2 (6–12 months) — Add the retention loop.** Layer **estimate-vs-actual job costing** + simple **job/status tracking** so quoting accuracy compounds and the product becomes the daily system of record. Add a free/cheap **floor "punch-in" tier** to capture labor actuals without the per-user resentment that drives MRPeasy complaints. **Benchmark:** watch monthly logo churn; if >3–4%/mo, the daily-use hook isn't sticking.

**Stage 3 (12–24 months) — Monetize compliance stickiness.** Add a **Compliance module** as a paid add-on: welder continuity tracking with lapse alerts, WPS/WPQ storage, mill-cert/MTR-to-job linkage with one-click audit packs, calibration due-dates, NCR/CAPA. Sell as "audit-ready," priced as a per-shop add-on (e.g., +$99–199/mo). **Benchmark:** if attach rate among customers with OEM/structural/ISO customers exceeds ~30%, double down; if not, keep it a checkbox feature.

**Do NOT build early:** full MRP/inventory, multi-level BOM automation, purchasing suites, heavy analytics, AI — these add the exact bloat and data-entry burden that drives abandonment, and they don't drive the initial purchase.

**Go-to-market:** earn trust (the biggest barrier for new software per owner forums) via transparent pricing, public demos, fast support, and proof in owner communities (Practical Machinist, r/Machinists, r/Welding, The Fabricator). Lead with the revenue message: "Quote faster, win more jobs, stop losing margin."

## Caveats
- **Forum/Reddit evidence is qualitative and self-selected** — vocal complainers and DIY-minded owners are overrepresented; many satisfied JobBOSS2/MRPeasy users exist (JobBOSS2 ~83% satisfaction across 1,000+ reviews). Most owner-voice quotes here are from Practical Machinist threads (Reddit threads were largely unindexed in search), which skews toward machine shops as well as fab/weld shops.
- **Several pricing figures are third-party estimates, not confirmed vendor pricing** (notably Prodsmart and ProShop dollar figures, which itqlick/softwarefinder explicitly label estimates); JobBOSS2/ProShop/Steelhead use quote-based pricing. Owner-reported numbers (JobBOSS2 $10–12K, ProShop ~$45–50K for 46 employees, Katana ~$899/mo) are more reliable than aggregator estimates but are anecdotal.
- **Vendor case-study metrics** (quote-time reductions, win-rate jumps like Flex 15%→68%) are marketing-sourced and reflect best cases, not typical results.
- **"Speed wins" statistics** (78% buy from first responder; 35–50% of deals to first responder) come from broad B2B/RFQ research (Lead Connect / industry analysts), not metal-fab-specific studies; directionally strong but not precise for this niche.
- **Compliance willingness-to-pay** is strongest among shops doing structural steel (AWS D1.1), pressure work (ASME), or serving OEM/aerospace/government customers; a pure job shop doing non-coded work may not value it — segment accordingly.
- **Market-size reports** vary widely by firm and methodology; treat absolute SMB-software dollar figures as rough, though the IoT Analytics MES figures are the most directly relevant and credible for this niche.
